Part Four • The Greater Story

Chapter Ten

Holiness That Heals

The healing of the woman with the issue of blood was far more than a miracle of physical restoration. It revealed the very heart of God. Hidden within this brief encounter is one of the clearest pictures of redemption found in the Gospels. In a single moment, Jesus demonstrated that His holiness was not something fragile that needed protection. It was a victorious holiness that overcame everything sin and suffering had brought into the world.

To appreciate the significance of this miracle, we must once again consider the ceremonial laws given to Israel in the book of Leviticus. These instructions were designed to teach God's people about holiness, purity, and reverence. They distinguished between what was ceremonially clean and ceremonially unclean, reminding Israel that the Lord is holy and that His covenant people were to approach Him with honor and obedience.

It is important to understand that ceremonial uncleanness was not the same as personal sin. A person could become ceremonially unclean through childbirth, disease, or contact with a dead body without having committed any moral wrongdoing. These laws pointed beyond themselves to humanity's greater need for spiritual cleansing, a need that could never be fully satisfied through rituals alone.

For twelve years the woman lived beneath the weight of those ceremonial regulations. Her condition affected where she could go, how others viewed her, and how she viewed herself. Every passing day reminded her that she was different. Every attempt to regain a normal life ended in disappointment. Her illness became a constant reminder of separation, isolation, and longing.

Then everything changed.

She reached through the crowd and touched the garment of Jesus.

According to the expectations of ceremonial law, many would have assumed that her uncleanness should pass to anyone she touched. Yet when her trembling hand reached the Savior, the exact opposite occurred. Uncleanness did not flow from her to Christ. Instead, healing flowed from Christ to her.

This remarkable reversal reveals the uniqueness of Jesus. Throughout His earthly ministry He repeatedly entered places where brokenness seemed to reign. He touched lepers whom others refused to approach. He welcomed children others considered unimportant. He spoke with those rejected by society, forgave sinners, comforted the grieving, and entered homes many religious leaders deliberately avoided. Wherever Jesus went, He moved toward human need rather than away from it.

Yet the brokenness He encountered never diminished His holiness. Sin never corrupted Him. Disease never defiled Him. Death itself could not overcome Him. Instead, His holiness transformed every life that responded to Him in faith.

This is one of the beautiful distinctions between religion and redemption. Human religion often emphasizes distance, creating barriers between the clean and the unclean, the acceptable and the rejected. Redemption through Christ accomplishes something entirely different. Jesus invites weary and broken people to come to Him exactly as they are because He alone possesses the power to cleanse, restore, and make them new.

The woman with the issue of blood did not become worthy before approaching Christ. She did not heal herself first. She did not earn His compassion. She came in weakness, trusting that Jesus could accomplish what she never could. Her faith rested not in His garment but in the One who wore it.

Her experience beautifully illustrates the message of the Gospel. Every person comes before God carrying a burden that cannot be removed through human effort. We cannot overcome sin by our own strength. We cannot earn forgiveness through good works. We cannot cleanse our own hearts. Like the woman in the crowd, we come empty handed, placing our confidence entirely in Jesus Christ.

The Law revealed humanity's need for cleansing, but Jesus became the fulfillment of everything the Law anticipated. Every sacrifice offered upon Israel's altars pointed toward the perfect sacrifice that would one day be offered upon the cross. Every act of ceremonial cleansing foreshadowed the greater cleansing accomplished through His blood.

The woman's healing therefore points beyond itself. It is not simply the story of one life restored. It is a picture of every believer who comes to Christ in faith. We come burdened by sin, and He offers forgiveness. We come spiritually empty, and He fills us with new life. We come separated from God, and through His grace we are welcomed into His family.

The miracle reminds us that holiness is not defeated by uncleanness. Rather, the holiness of Christ overcomes everything that sin has broken. His light drives out darkness. His life conquers death. His righteousness covers our guilt. His grace reaches where no human effort can ever reach.

The woman discovered this truth the moment she touched Jesus. What twelve years of suffering could not accomplish was completed in an instant by the Savior's power. Her healing was not simply the end of a disease. It was a living testimony that the presence of Christ changes everything it touches.

The same remains true today.

Jesus still welcomes the weary.

He still restores the broken.

He still cleanses the sinner.

He still calls sons and daughters into His family.

His holiness has never been overcome.

It is still healing hearts, restoring lives, and accomplishing the work of redemption for all who come to Him in faith.
