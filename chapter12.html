Part Four • The Greater Story

Chapter Twelve

Remembered Forever

The Gospel writers never tell us her name.

We know nothing about the town where she lived, the family into which she was born, or what became of her after the day Jesus healed her. Scripture remains silent concerning many of the details we naturally wish it had preserved. Yet perhaps that silence serves a greater purpose.

Had the Bible recorded her name, readers might have focused upon the woman herself. Instead, every generation has been invited to focus upon the Savior who changed her life. Her identity became secondary because Christ became central.

For nearly two thousand years, believers around the world have remembered her simply as the woman with the issue of blood. Although her name has been forgotten by history, her faith has never been forgotten by the Church.

This is one of the beautiful paradoxes found throughout Scripture. Many of history's kings, rulers, and military commanders are remembered only by historians, while ordinary men and women who placed their faith in God continue to inspire countless believers across the centuries.

Jesus once said that whoever humbles himself will be exalted. The woman who tried to disappear unnoticed into the crowd became one of the most beloved examples of faith in the New Testament.

Long after the apostles preached throughout the Roman Empire, early Christian tradition preserved an interesting account concerning her life. According to the fourth century church historian Eusebius, a bronze statue stood in Caesarea Philippi depicting a woman kneeling before Christ with her hands stretched toward Him in gratitude. Although Scripture itself does not mention this monument, many early Christians believed it commemorated the woman whom Jesus healed.

Whether the tradition is historically accurate cannot be known with certainty. Like many traditions handed down through the centuries, it should be approached with humility and careful discernment. It is an interesting piece of Christian history, but it does not carry the authority of Holy Scripture.

Even if such a statue truly existed, it was never her greatest memorial.

Bronze eventually corrodes.

Stone slowly crumbles.

Buildings fall.

Empires disappear.

The Word of God endures forever.

The greatest monument to this woman's life is not found in metal or marble. It is found within the pages of Scripture, where the Holy Spirit preserved her testimony for every generation that would follow. Millions of believers have read her story. Countless sermons have been preached from her example. Her faith continues to encourage those who quietly wonder whether God still sees their pain.

Her legacy is not that she suffered.

Her legacy is that she believed.

She reminds us that genuine faith often grows in seasons of prolonged hardship. Twelve years of unanswered questions did not destroy her confidence in God. Instead, those difficult years prepared her for the moment she encountered Jesus.

Every generation has people who identify with her story. Some carry chronic illnesses. Others live with emotional wounds that few people understand. Many struggle with loneliness, rejection, disappointment, or the feeling that life has passed them by. The details may differ, but the invitation of Christ remains the same.

Come.

Come with your questions.

Come with your weakness.

Come with your fears.

Come with your brokenness.

Come to Jesus.

The woman reached for the hem of His garment believing He could change her life.

He did.

Her body was healed.

Her dignity was restored.

Her fear was replaced with peace.

Her isolation gave way to acceptance.

Most importantly, she left knowing who she was.

She was not defined by twelve years of suffering.

She was defined by the word Jesus spoke over her life.

"Daughter."

That single word echoes far beyond the streets of Galilee. It continues to speak into the lives of men and women who wonder whether God still knows their names. The Gospel reminds us that Christ does not define us by our failures, our wounds, or the labels the world places upon us. He calls us according to His grace.

The woman's story is ultimately not about one miraculous healing.

It is about the faithfulness of God.

It is about the compassion of Jesus.

It is about the power of redemption.

It is about the Savior who still stops for forgotten people.

As long as the Gospel is proclaimed, her testimony will continue to point beyond herself to the One who made her whole.

History may never remember her earthly name.

Heaven certainly does.

And because of Jesus Christ, her story will be remembered forever.
